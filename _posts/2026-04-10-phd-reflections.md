As I approach the end of my PhD, I've been reflecting on the journey — not just the research, but the personal growth that came with it.

## The First Year

Starting a PhD is simultaneously exciting and terrifying. You go from being a strong student to feeling like you know nothing. Imposter syndrome hits hard when everyone around you seems to understand things you've never heard of.

What helped me was accepting that **not knowing things is the whole point**. A PhD is about pushing the boundary of human knowledge, which means spending most of your time in the unknown.

## Finding Your Research Direction

One of the hardest parts is finding a research direction that is both meaningful and feasible. I changed topics multiple times in my first two years. Looking back, each "failed" direction taught me something valuable.

My advice: don't commit too early, but also don't explore forever. At some point, you need to go deep.

## The Middle Years

The middle of a PhD is where most people struggle. The initial excitement fades, and the end feels far away. Papers get rejected. Experiments fail. You question your choices.

What I learned: **progress is not linear**. There are long stretches where nothing seems to work, followed by breakthroughs. The key is to keep showing up.

## Mental Health

I won't sugarcoat it — the PhD has been hard on my mental health at times. The pressure to publish, the uncertainty about the future, and the isolation can take a toll.

Some things that helped me:

- Setting boundaries with work
- Regular exercise
- Talking to friends outside of academia
- Remembering that my worth is not defined by my publication count

## What I Would Tell My Past Self

1. **It's okay to ask for help.** Struggling in silence is not a badge of honor.
2. **Take breaks.** Your best ideas will come when you're not staring at a screen.
3. **Build relationships.** The people you meet matter more than the papers you publish.
4. **Be kind to yourself.** You're doing something incredibly hard, and that's worth acknowledging.

The PhD has been the most challenging and rewarding experience of my life. I'm grateful for every part of it — even the hard parts.
